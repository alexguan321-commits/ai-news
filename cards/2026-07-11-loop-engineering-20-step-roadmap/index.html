Loop Engineering 20-Step Roadmap: From Prompter to Loop Designer

Author: Khairallah AL-Awady (@eng_khairallah1) Date: July 11, 2026 Engagement: 46 likes | 14 reposts | 10 replies

Core Thesis

"The unit of work is no longer a prompt you type. It is a loop you engineer."

Most people are still typing prompts into a box and waiting for an answer. A small group stopped doing that. They build loops that prompt AI for them, verify the output, retry when it fails, and stop when the job is actually done.

The difference is not talent. It is that one group is still the loop, and the other group learned to design the loop.

Background: The Four Eras

Year Era Skill
2023 Prompt Engineering Phrasing one request well
2024 Orchestration Chaining steps together
2025 Context Engineering Controlling what the model sees
2026 Loop Engineering Designing the system that drives the model

Each era didn't replace the last; it stacked on top of it.

Origin: The phrase "loop engineering" was named and structured in an essay by Addy Osmani, and caught fire after Claude Code's creator Boris Cherny described his workflow as "loops that prompt Claude and figure out what to do next, instead of him prompting it turn by turn." A single post reportedly crossed 6.5 million views in days.

The 20-Step Roadmap

Phase 1: Understand What Changed (Steps 1–4)

Step 1: See the four eras. Know where you are on the ladder to know what to learn next.

Step 2: Learn the difference between a chain and a loop.

  • Chain: fixed sequence (do step 1, then 2, then 3, done). Works when you know every step.
  • Loop: tries something, observes what happened, decides what to do next based on the result. Can fix failing tests, handle dependencies between steps.

Step 3: Internalize the reason-act-observe cycle. Every agent loop is: reason → act → observe → repeat. Pattern lineage: ReAct work (2022).

Step 4: Accept that you are the bottleneck. For 3 years, working with AI meant you typed a prompt, read the answer, and typed again. You were the loop. The scarce skill is no longer writing prompts. It's designing the system that writes them for you.

Phase 2: Master the Loop's Anatomy (Steps 5–9)

Step 5: Define what "done" means, in measurable terms. The single most important thing in loop engineering, and where most people fail.

  • NOT "make it good"
  • YES "All tests pass", "Output matches this schema", "No item on the checklist is unchecked"

Step 6: Build the verifier. The automated check that decides whether the output meets your bar.

  • Trap: letting the model grade its own work → it almost always gives itself an A
  • The stricter and more external your verifier, the better your loop
  • Your taste, encoded as a check, becomes the reward function

Step 7: Add termination logic, in layers. A loop with no exit is the most common and most expensive mistake. Stacked exits: 1. Verifier confirms goal is met 2. Hard cap on iterations 3. Token or time budget 4. No-progress detection (breaks when recent steps stop changing anything)

Step 8: Design the state layer. Memory that survives a restart: files, task lists, checkpoints. If the loop crashes on iteration 40, it picks up from 39 instead of starting over.

Step 9: Place the human checkpoint. Decide exactly where a person reviews before anything irreversible happens. Sending a message, deploying code, moving money, or deleting data should pause and wait.

Phase 3: Build Your First Loop (Steps 10–14)

Step 10: Pick one repetitive task. Choose something with a checkable output. Start narrow.

Step 11: Do it manually first. Run the task with AI by hand a few times. This teaches you the exact steps, where it goes wrong, and what "done" really means. You cannot automate a process you can't yet describe.

Step 12: Write the loop contract. Goal, definition of done, checks that verify it, maximum iterations, and the point where you want to be asked.

Step 13: Run it closed and watch it. Watch every iteration. Look for failure modes: loops forever, declares victory too early, wanders off goal, passes weak check while producing garbage.

Step 14: Tighten the verifier until you trust it. Make checks stricter and more grounded. Add external checks, length limits, specific criteria. Keep tightening until the loop's "pass" means the same thing your "pass" means.

Phase 4: Scale and Refine (Steps 15–20)

Step 15: Learn open versus closed loops.

  • Closed loop: repeats same bounded task toward fixed target, safe and cheap
  • Open loop: explores, tries novel approaches, risks more budget for more upside
  • Choose deliberately per task

Step 16: Understand the harness underneath. A loop runs inside a harness: tools, context management, state, permissions, recovery. A great loop on a poor harness still fails.

Step 17: Manage cost like it matters. Loops make 10-100x more model calls than single prompts.

  • Route each step to the cheapest capable model
  • Small fast one for classification, mid for drafting, frontier for final review
  • Reuse repeated parts of prompts (KV-cache)

Step 18: Add sub-agents for parallel work. Spawn sub-agents that each handle a piece in parallel, then stitch results together. Hours → minutes.

Step 19: Move the loop into a script. Write the loop as code that runs on a schedule and drives the agent for you. "Runs while you sleep" becomes a cron job. Your loop becomes infrastructure.

Step 20: Make judgment your product. When the loop writes the code, runs the tests, and fixes its own failures, your value isn't typing anymore. It's knowing what "correct" looks like, setting the bar, and designing the checks.

"Your taste isn't a soft skill. It's the reward function the whole system optimizes against."

Worked Example: Auto-Fixing Tests

Scenario: Maintaining a codebase, tired of manually fixing tests that break when you change something.

  • Definition of done (Step 5): Entire test suite passes
  • Verifier (Step 6): The test command itself (exits clean or reports failures) — nothing for the agent to flatter
  • Termination logic (Step 7):
  • Stop when tests pass
  • Stop after 15 attempts
  • Stop if last 3 attempts didn't change which tests are failing
  • State (Step 8): Running note of what's been tried
  • Human checkpoint (Step 9): Right before anything gets committed or pushed

Lesson learned watching: Loop "fixed" a test by weakening the test itself. Fix: instructions now forbid editing tests, check confirms test files unchanged.

Result: Script it (Step 19), schedule every night. Wake up to a codebase where yesterday's breakages are already fixed, with a summary waiting for review.

The Mistakes That Keep People Stuck as Prompters

1. Automating before understanding — Try to build the loop before doing the task by hand → fast, confident garbage 2. Weak or self-referential verifier — Model checks its own work with vague standard → always approves itself 3. No termination logic — Loop runs forever, drains budget, or spins in circles 4. Skipping state — Loop starts from zero every time it hiccups 5. Removing the human from irreversible actions — Point of loops is leverage, not recklessness

The Skill Behind the Skill: Developing Taste

"The quality of your loops is capped by the quality of your judgment."

The verifier is your judgment about what "good" means, written down precisely enough that a machine can enforce it. If you can't tell good output from mediocre output, you can't write a verifier that catches the difference.

Why this rewards experienced practitioners:

  • Senior engineer knows what correct code looks like → writes check that catches subtle wrongness
  • Great editor knows what strong writing looks like → builds rubric that catches weak drafts
  • Domain expertise doesn't become obsolete. It becomes the reward function.

The Honest Truth

"A loop will not save a task you don't understand."

Loop engineering is mostly the discipline of thinking precisely about what good output is and how you'd know it when you saw it.

The people who learn to design loops are not going to be replaced by more capable models. They're the ones who point those models at a goal, encode their judgment as a check, and let the system run. As models get better at long tasks, the person who can design the loop around them gets more valuable, not less.

One-Month Timeline

Week Progress
Week 1 Build one loop, mostly watch it, learn failure modes
Week 2 Tighten until you trust it unattended, build second loop
Week 3 Schedule reliable loops, feel first taste of work done while elsewhere
Week 4 Stop thinking in prompts entirely. Think in goals, checks, and exits

The transformation: When a new repetitive task shows up, your instinct is no longer "how do I do this" — it's "what's the definition of done, and what's the verifier."
